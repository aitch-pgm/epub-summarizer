Ingrid Hu Dahl is the first mixed-race girl in her family. Dahl's parents lived with real cultural and racial challenges and backlash. They navigated harsh assumptions. My parents' interracial match shocked both sets of families. Ma was Taiwanese, of Chinese descent; Ba was American, of European and Scandinavian descent. Back in Taiwan, my mother’s family referred to Dad as a “white barbarian’ My parents' truth was an act of bravery, a declaration of sorts, but also one that would always have a spotlight. Their life together would be a spectacle, open to ridicule, envy, and unwanted glares and judgmental gazes. As a young couple, they pushed aside these pressures with a bravery I admire. Every other year or so, we would get the opportunity to fly to Taipei, Taiwan, and visit Nai Nai and family. These trips infused my life with color and instilled in me a taste for adventure, culture, and language. In Taiwan during our visits, all family members were expected to attend dinner at Nai Nai’s home most nights. She had taught her hired help how to make her favorite dishes for breakfast, lunch, and dinner. “Tian fan!” she’d roar from deep within her diaphragm. My aunt Nancy was Nai Nai’s favorite, even though Ma was the eldest. Nancy was a film star, singer, and model who had a master's degree in finance. They would sit for hours at the circular dinner table, laughing and talking, leaving Mom the outsider, always wanting. At the age of six, Jude's confidence got hard knocked when she fell down the stairs at her mother's grocery store. Jude's mother, Ma, was seven months pregnant with her baby brother at the time. Jude: "I was frozen momentarily before I, too, ran over" My white grandma, whom I called “Gram,” cooked, sewed, and drove with a lead foot. She had grown up during the Depression in a poor and abusive household in a family of six children. She was pulled out of school in the eighth grade and expected to work and hand her paychecks to her father. Ingrid's grandmother had a strained relationship with her father and mother's marriage. She noticed the strange way she looked at Nai Nai, but, as a kid, she didn't recognize or understand it. One day, she became increasingly animated while watching something on TV. On Sunday evenings, between 1987 and 1994, my family and I would wait for the new episode of TNG to air on television. TNG introduced us to a place no one had gone before. During the series, audiences were introduced to androgynous characters.

Chapter 26: Chapter 26

Ma died a day before turning sixty-seven. She spent two years living with an oxygen machine, its brightly colored tubes following her every step. Ma had held on for a month past what hospice professional thought possible. Ma’s head had tilted to the side. She was drooling, her body following the weight of her skull as if diving into water. Ba expressed relief that neither of us had to witness what was left of her frail body turning an eerie shade of pale gray. After saying his goodbyes to his friends, family, and a life that was always tethered to Ma, Ba boarded the long plane ride home with her remaining half. Ba moved back to the fifty-five-and-up community in New Jersey that he had once lived in with Ma. Ba said that without Ma, his life was like a poster with a big gaping hole in the center. Parixit and Dane were there, honoring me during this tender moment. Their presence reminded me that I was loved and made me reflect on my journey. Ba read a passage from the Bible. He honored Ma to his fullest, just as she had for Nai Nai. The morning after Ma’s funeral and celebration of life event, I woke up with a tingling sensation, picturing a stunning phoenix circling above the house we were staying in. She was free and she was everywhere. “Ma Bao Bei,” I called out to her in my mind, “I love you.”
